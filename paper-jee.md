abstract: |
  The credibility revolution has made research design central to empirical economics, but undergraduate and graduate econometrics is still largely taught as a sequence of point estimates, leaving students with the misimpression that a coefficient is "the" answer rather than one defensible choice among many. This paper develops a teaching approach that confronts students directly with analytic flexibility, using panel data—where the choice of estimator encodes a sharp and well-understood identification trade-off—as the vehicle. The pedagogy is organized around the specification curve: students take a single estimand on a single real dataset and construct the entire distribution of estimates implied by reasonable choices of estimator, controls, sample, and inference. Applied to a classic wage panel, the approach yields a clean and replicable lesson with genuine substantive content: the union and marriage wage premiums roughly halve once fixed effects absorb time-invariant heterogeneity (the signature of selection), while the return to schooling cannot be identified by within estimators at all. I argue that this "universe-first" framing teaches identification and the limits of inference more effectively than the estimator-by-estimator tradition, situate it within the literatures on research transparency and active learning in economics, and provide a sample assignment and assessment scheme. # Introduction

The credibility revolution reorganized empirical economics around research design and the careful defense of identifying assumptions [@angrist2010credibility]. Curricula have followed, devoting ever more attention to applied microeconometrics. Yet a gap remains between how empirical economics is practiced and how it is taught. Practicing researchers know that an estimate is the endpoint of a long chain of analytic decisions—which estimator, which controls, which sample, which standard errors—and that many of those decisions are defensible yet consequential. Students, working through a curriculum that presents one estimator and one preferred specification at a time, too often leave with the opposite intuition: that the data deliver a single number, and that the econometrician's job is to compute it correctly.

This gap is not new, and naming it has a distinguished history. Leamer [-@leamer1983] urged the profession to "take the con out of econometrics" by reporting how sensitive estimates are to specification rather than presenting a single equation as if it were inevitable. The concern has only grown more pressing as evidence of specification searching and selective reporting has accumulated in published economics [@brodeur2016; @christensen2018], and as methodologists have given the idea operational form: the *garden of forking paths* [@gelman2013], *multiverse analysis* [@steegen2016], and *specification-curve analysis* [@simonsohn2020] all propose reporting the distribution of estimates across the space of reasonable analyses instead of a single cherry-picked result. Teaching students to think this way—to see an estimate as a point in a distribution they themselves could have shifted—is among the most valuable and most durable lessons an econometrics course can impart. It is also among the hardest to teach, because constructing such a distribution requires programming fluency that beginning students do not yet have, so the lesson is typically deferred until after the habit of the single estimate has already formed.

This paper makes three contributions. First, it develops a *teaching approach*—a "universe-first" framing in which the object of study is the entire specification curve rather than any individual regression—and argues that panel data are the ideal vehicle for it, because the menu of panel estimators encodes a sharp identification trade-off that a single real dataset can make concrete. Second, it provides a *worked, reproducible demonstration* on a classic wage panel that doubles as a substantive result: the fragility of the union and marriage premiums under fixed effects, and the non-identification of the schooling return, are shown to be robust features of the data rather than artifacts of one analysis, and the paper supplies a sample assignment and assessment scheme for classroom use. Third, it takes *verification* seriously: because a teaching tool that produced subtly wrong numbers would be worse than useless, every estimate in the demonstration is independently reproduced in two languages. A free browser-based implementation accompanies the paper and removes the programming barrier for students, but it is the instrument of the approach, not its substance.

# The credibility revolution reaches the classroom

Two literatures motivate the approach, and the gap between them defines its contribution. The first is the scholarship on research transparency. Beyond Leamer's [-@leamer1983] original critique, the last decade has produced both systematic evidence and conceptual tools. Brodeur et al. [-@brodeur2016] document bunching of test statistics just past conventional significance thresholds in leading economics journals, consistent with specification searching; Christensen and Miguel [-@christensen2018] survey the resulting transparency agenda—pre-registration, replication, and the reporting of robustness—across the discipline. The methodological response has been to formalize "researcher degrees of freedom" [@simmons2011] and to recommend reporting the *whole* distribution of defensible estimates: the multiverse analysis of Steegen et al. [-@steegen2016] and the specification-curve analysis of Simonsohn et al. [-@simonsohn2020]. These are now part of the working vocabulary of empirical social science, and graduates entering research are expected to understand them.

The second literature concerns how economics is actually taught. Becker and Watts [-@beckerwatts1996] documented that instruction remained overwhelmingly "chalk and talk," and their follow-up surveys found that, while the dominance of the lecture eased only slowly, active and computer-based methods were spreading [@becker2000; @wattsschaur2011]. The accumulated case, synthesized in the handbook literature on teaching economics [@hoytmcgoldrick2012], is that students learn empirical reasoning best by doing it—by manipulating data and confronting the consequences of their own analytic choices—rather than by watching a single derivation unfold on the board.

The gap is that the first literature's central lesson is rarely delivered through the second literature's preferred mode. Analytic flexibility is taught, when it is taught, as an admonition ("report robustness checks") rather than as an experience, because giving students the experience has required them to program a specification curve before they have the skills to do so. The approach developed here closes that gap: it turns the abstract injunction to consider the multiverse into a concrete, hands-on exercise that beginning students can perform, and it does so in the part of the curriculum—panel data—where the lesson is sharpest.

# Analytic flexibility as a teaching problem

The pedagogical case for a universe-first approach rests on a simple observation: the spread of estimates across defensible analyses carries information that no single estimate can. Three questions become both askable and answerable once the whole specification curve is in view, and each maps onto a concept that econometrics courses already aim to teach.

The first is *how much the headline number moves across defensible choices*—the width of the curve. This operationalizes Leamer's [-@leamer1983] fragility critique and gives students a concrete, visual criterion for how much confidence a literature's "consensus estimate" deserves. The second is *which choices move it.* When the estimates are arranged alongside the analytic decisions behind them, students can read the drivers directly: if the low estimates are systematically the fixed-effects estimates and the high ones the pooled estimates, they have discovered selection on time-invariant heterogeneity for themselves, inductively, rather than receiving it as a definition. The third is *whether the question can be answered at all under some choices.* Identification—the deepest and least intuitive idea in the panel-data curriculum—becomes visceral when a third of a specification curve simply disappears because the chosen estimator cannot speak to a time-invariant regressor.

Panel data are uniquely suited to carry this lesson because their estimator menu is not a set of interchangeable options but a set of positions on an identification spectrum. Pooled OLS uses all variation in the data; the between estimator uses only cross-individual variation in individual means; the within (fixed-effects) estimator discards all between-individual variation and identifies effects purely from within-individual change, thereby purging all time-invariant confounding—observed or not—at the cost of being unable to estimate anything that does not vary within an individual [@mundlak1978; @wooldridge2010]. Random effects occupies an intermediate position under a stronger exogeneity assumption, and the Hausman [-@hausman1978] test formalizes the choice between it and fixed effects. A student who watches a coefficient fall monotonically as the estimator moves along this spectrum has learned what the spectrum *means*, not merely how to compute each point on it.

# A reproducible demonstration

To show that the approach yields a substantive lesson rather than a contrived one, I apply it to the wage panel of Vella and Verbeek [-@vellaverbeek1998], a balanced sample of 545 young men from the U.S. National Longitudinal Survey of Youth observed annually from 1980 to 1987 and distributed in the `wooldridge` and `plm` R packages [@wooldridge2010pkg; @croissant2008]. The dataset is small, clean, and a long-standing teaching staple; it supports three estimands that together tell a complete story about selection and identification.

*The union and marriage premiums.* Regressing log hourly wages on union status and a quadratic in experience, the estimated union premium is about 0.167 under pooled OLS but only about 0.083 under fixed effects—it roughly halves once stable individual characteristics are removed. The marriage premium behaves identically, falling from about 0.164 to 0.047. The interpretation is the textbook one, but here it is *discovered* rather than asserted: the men who are unionized or married differ systematically from those who are not, in ways that pooled OLS misattributes to union status or marriage. The between estimator, which relies entirely on cross-individual comparisons, returns the *largest* estimates of all (about 0.257 and 0.220), localizing the inflation precisely in the between-individual variation that fixed effects discard.

*The return to schooling.* Switching the estimand to years of schooling produces the lesson's turn. Pooled OLS, the between estimator, and random effects all return a familiar return of roughly 0.10 per year of schooling, but the fixed-effects and first-difference estimators return nothing at all: schooling does not change within these men over 1980–1987, so the within transformation annihilates it. Half the universe of specifications becomes unidentifiable. Few classroom moments make the meaning of within-identification—and the price fixed effects exact for its robustness—as concrete as watching those specifications vanish.

These contrasts are summarized in Figure 1, the estimator "spectrum" across all three estimands. For the union and marriage premiums the estimates fall monotonically as the estimator moves from pooled and between, through random effects, to within and first differences, and the only statistically insignificant estimates sit at the fixed-effects/first-difference end. For the return to schooling the fixed-effects and first-difference columns are simply empty: the estimator cannot speak to a regressor that does not move within a person. A single figure thus delivers two of the course's hardest ideas at once—selection on unobservables and the identification cost of within transformations.

*The full curve.* Opening the universe over estimators, control sets, the inclusion of year effects, and sample restrictions for the union premium yields the specification curve in Figure 2: 160 estimates running from roughly 0.04 to 0.26, with a median near 0.09, almost no sign reversals, and a transparent ordering. The high end is populated by pooled and between specifications; the low end—including the only statistically insignificant estimates—by within and first-difference specifications. The defensible reading is neither "the union premium is 0.17" nor "the union premium is 0.08," but a positive and modest premium whose precise magnitude is genuinely uncertain and turns on what one is willing to assume about selection. Teaching students to *state that conclusion*, rather than to defend a single number, is the point of the exercise.

# Bringing it into the classroom

The approach is designed for the point in an intermediate-undergraduate or graduate course at which panel estimators are introduced. It assumes students have seen multiple regression and the idea of omitted-variable bias, but it does not assume they can program; that is precisely its advantage over a code-first treatment, because it lets the conceptual lesson precede, rather than wait on, the acquisition of software skills. The approach directly targets two persistent misconceptions: that more controls always mean a "better" estimate (the curve shows controls often barely move the focal coefficient, while the choice of estimator dominates), and that statistical significance is a property of a finding rather than of a specification (the curve shows the same estimand moving in and out of significance as defensible choices change).

Three modes of use have guided its design. As a *lecture demonstration*, the instructor introduces each estimator while the corresponding region of the spectrum (Figure 1) or curve (Figure 2) is highlighted, then steps back to the whole picture to motivate the discussion of selection and identification in a single image. As a *guided exercise*, students predict the shape of the curve before constructing it and then explain what they see. As a *bridge to programming*, students in courses that teach R, Stata, or Python reproduce one specification from the curve in code, using the demonstration as an answer key and connecting point-and-click intuition to reproducible practice [@ballmedeiros2012].

### A sample assignment

The following assignment, which students can complete in roughly an hour, has organized my own use of the approach.

1. **Predict.** Before computing anything, write down your guess for the union wage premium and whether you expect it to be larger under pooled OLS or under fixed effects. Justify the direction in one sentence.
2. **Build the universe.** Construct the specification curve for the union premium over all five estimators, the available controls, the inclusion of year effects, and the three sample definitions. Report the median, the range, and the share of specifications in which the premium is statistically significant.
3. **Explain the ordering.** Using the choice dashboard, identify which analytic decision most moves the estimate. Explain, in terms of within- versus between-individual variation, why fixed-effects specifications cluster at one end and pooled specifications at the other.
4. **Probe identification.** Repeat step 2 for the return to schooling. Why do the fixed-effects and first-difference specifications disappear? What does this imply about what panel data can and cannot tell us about the returns to education?
5. **Report responsibly.** Write the one-paragraph conclusion you would defend to a skeptical reader—stating a range and the assumption on which the upper and lower ends depend—rather than a single number.

A simple rubric assesses (i) the correctness of the computed range and significance share, (ii) a correct mechanical explanation of the within–between ordering, (iii) a correct account of why schooling is not identified under within estimators, and (iv) the quality of the final, distribution-aware interpretation. The emphasis throughout is on interpretation and the defense of analytic choices, not on arriving at a single "right" coefficient.

Because the barrier to this kind of active, data-driven instruction has historically been the setup and programming cost that pushes it out of the classroom [@beckerwatts1996; @becker2000], the demonstration is accompanied by a free, open-source application that runs entirely in a web browser, bundles the data, and requires no installation or coding. It presents the data (including a within-versus-between variance decomposition that previews why fixed effects can estimate the union premium but not the schooling return), builds the specification curve interactively, and allows any single specification to be opened in full with its regression table, Hausman test, and residual diagnostics. The application lowers the cost of adopting the approach to essentially zero, but the approach—and the lesson it delivers—does not depend on it.

# Relation to existing tools and approaches

Specification-curve and multiverse analyses can be produced in research code—for example with the `specr` package in R—but such tools presuppose the programming fluency that the present approach is designed to precede; they are instruments for researchers, not first encounters for students. Panel estimation is taught with `plm` [@croissant2008] or Stata's `xtreg`, again code-first, and with the single-specification framing this paper seeks to displace. Web-based statistics applets exist but typically rely on simulated data, which lets students dismiss inconvenient results as artifacts of the simulation rather than features of the world; grounding the demonstration in a real, well-known panel removes that escape and lends the lesson its force. The contribution here is therefore neither a new estimator nor merely another applet, but the pairing of a universe-first pedagogy of identification with a real-data demonstration whose every number is independently verified, delivered in a form that requires no programming.

# Reproducibility and verification

A teaching tool that produced subtly incorrect estimates would mislead precisely the students it aims to help, so the computational pipeline is held to a research standard of reproducibility. The estimators are implemented from textbook formulas and verified at three levels. First, an engine self-test reproduces canonical `plm` values on benchmark specifications, including the coefficients and standard errors of all five estimators, the Hausman statistic, and the non-identification of time-invariant regressors under within transformations. Second and third, *every* estimate in the demonstration—all 1,440 specifications spanning the three estimands and the full grid of analytic choices—is independently re-fit in two languages: R, using the `plm` package [@croissant2008], and Python, using `linearmodels`. Across all 1,440 specifications, both implementations reproduce the application's estimates: the pooled-OLS, between, fixed-effects, and first-difference coefficients agree to better than $1\times10^{-6}$ (R) and $5\times10^{-7}$ (Python), and the random-effects coefficients to $3\times10^{-4}$, the small residual reflecting alternative implementations of the Swamy–Arora variance-component estimator. The application, its source, the bundled data, and all three verification scripts are openly available, consistent with the expectation that the data and computations underlying published work be shared. Instructors can therefore adopt the approach knowing that the numbers their students see have been checked, independently and in full, against the field's reference implementations.

# Limitations and extensions

Three limitations bound the contribution. First, the demonstration uses a single, deliberately simple panel; its purpose is to teach reasoning transferable to other settings, not to establish a fact about union or marriage premiums, and instructors should be explicit that the substantive estimates are illustrative. Second, the approach complements rather than replaces instruction in statistical programming: its role is to build the conceptual scaffolding—what a specification curve is, what it reveals, and how to read identification from it—onto which coding skills are later attached. Third, random-effects estimates depend on the variance-component estimator chosen, and although the implementation matches the standard Swamy–Arora results to within $3\times10^{-4}$, instructors should treat random effects as one defensible choice rather than a canonical benchmark. The natural extension is to other research designs whose credibility likewise turns on analytic choices—difference-in-differences, instrumental variables, and event studies—each of which admits its own multiverse and could be taught in the same universe-first spirit.

# Concluding remarks

Teaching students to run a regression is no longer enough. The empirical turn in economics requires that they also learn to read an estimate as one point in a space of analyses, to see which assumptions move it, and to report that space honestly. Panel data make this lesson teachable because the estimator menu encodes a real and consequential identification trade-off, and a single classic dataset turns that trade-off into something students can see: premiums that halve under fixed effects, and a schooling return that vanishes. The contribution of this paper is that universe-first approach and its verified, reproducible demonstration; the accompanying browser tool simply removes the last barrier to bringing it into the classroom. Together they offer a concrete answer to a long-standing call—to move beyond chalk and talk, and to take the con out of how we teach econometrics.
